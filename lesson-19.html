How close elections actually get
Tap each one to see the margin. These are all verified real elections.
2020
U.S. Presidential Election — decided by three states
▶
42,918 votes
Joe Biden received over 7 million more popular votes than Donald Trump. But the election was decided by the Electoral College — specifically by three states: Georgia (11,779 votes), Arizona (10,457 votes), and Wisconsin (20,682 votes). Biden won the presidency by a combined margin of 42,918 votes across those three states — slightly more than the average attendance at a Dodgers game.
The presidency. Four years of policy, cabinet appointments, Supreme Court nominations, executive orders, and the direction of every federal agency.
2000
U.S. Presidential Election — Florida recount
▶
537 votes
George W. Bush won the state of Florida — and the presidency — by 537 votes out of nearly 6 million cast. The margin was 0.009% of the Florida vote. The election dragged on for 36 days of recounts and legal battles before the Supreme Court ended it. That 537-vote margin determined who became president of the United States.
The presidency — including the decision to invade Iraq, the response to 9/11, and two Supreme Court appointments.
2008
Minnesota U.S. Senate Race — Al Franken
▶
312 votes
Democrat Al Franken defeated Republican Norm Coleman by 312 votes out of nearly 2.9 million cast in Minnesota's Senate race. The margin was 0.01% of all votes cast. The race required multiple recounts and a legal battle lasting nearly 8 months. Franken's eventual win gave Democrats a 60-vote supermajority in the Senate — the number needed to break a filibuster.
That 60th Senate seat allowed Democrats to pass the Affordable Care Act — which extended health coverage to tens of millions of Americans.
2017
Virginia House of Delegates — tied, decided by drawing
▶
0 votes (tie)
A Virginia state House race ended in a perfect tie out of more than 23,000 votes cast. The tie was broken by literally drawing a name out of a bowl — a film canister with the Republican candidate's name was pulled. That single seat gave Republicans control of the entire state House by one seat — changing which party controlled Virginia's legislature.
One state legislative seat — decided by chance — determined party control of an entire state legislature and shaped two years of state policy.
2016
Vermont State Senate primary — decided by 1 vote
▶
1 vote
A Vermont state Senate Democratic primary was determined by a single vote out of more than 7,400 cast. One person's decision to show up — or not — was literally the margin. Local and state primaries like this happen constantly and almost no one votes in them, which means the few who do have enormous influence.
State senators control education funding, criminal justice law, tax policy, and hundreds of other decisions that affect daily life in ways most people do not realize.
What local offices actually control
Most people vote for president and ignore everything else. But the officials closest to you control the most immediate parts of your daily life. Tap each one to see what they actually do.
🏫
School Board
Tap to see what they control
Controls curriculum, school budgets, teacher pay, school safety policies, library books, and whether schools in your area are well-funded or not. School board elections often have turnout in the single digits — a small number of people make decisions for every student in the district.
⚖️
County Sheriff
Tap to see what they control
The sheriff is the chief law enforcement officer of a county. They decide how police interact with the community, what gets prioritized, how arrests are made, and how the county jail is run. In rural Kansas, the sheriff is often the most powerful law enforcement officer most people ever interact with.
🏛️
County Commissioner
Tap to see what they control
Controls county roads, property taxes, public health, local zoning and land use, the county budget, and local social services. In many rural Kansas counties, the county commission makes decisions that directly affect thousands of people — with almost no one paying attention.
🏙️
Mayor / City Council
Tap to see what they control
Controls local police department, city budget, roads and infrastructure, zoning (where businesses and housing can go), local parks and services, and how the city responds to emergencies. The mayor and city council make decisions that affect your neighborhood directly and immediately.
👨‍⚖️
Local Judges
Tap to see what they control
In Kansas, some judges face retention votes — you vote yes or no on whether to keep them. Local judges handle criminal cases, family court, civil disputes, and juvenile matters. The judge who presides over cases in your community is often elected — and almost no one knows anything about them when they vote.
🏦
State Legislature
Tap to see what they control
State legislators control state income tax rates, Medicaid funding, education funding, minimum wage, abortion law, gun laws, criminal sentencing, and redistricting — which determines how Congressional and state districts are drawn. State elections directly shape the laws you live under every day.
Midterms matter as much as presidential elections
Most people show up for presidential elections and disappear for everything else. Here is why that is a problem.
47%
Youth turnout
2024 presidential election (ages 18-29)
23%
Youth turnout
2022 midterm election (ages 18-29)
When young people do not vote in midterms, the electorate skews older — and older voters have very different priorities than younger ones. Midterms determine which party controls Congress, which controls the budget, which confirms judges, and which sets the agenda for the next two years. Presidential elections get the attention. Midterms get the power.
⚠️ In 2014 — the lowest youth turnout midterm in recent history — only 13% of eligible voters ages 18-29 cast a ballot. In some states it was under 10%. Meaning roughly 9 out of 10 young people sat out an election that determined control of the entire Congress.
The gap between presidential and midterm youth turnout is not random. It is partly that midterms get less attention, partly that there is less outreach to young voters in off years, and partly that people underestimate how much a midterm election controls. But the gap also means that young voters have less influence in the elections that happen more often — and that is a problem that compounds over time.
How to actually vote in Kansas
Everything you need to know — specific to Kansas.
📅
You must be registered 21 days before Election Day to vote. Kansas does not have same-day registration — so plan ahead.
🪪
You must show a valid photo ID to vote in person. A driver's license, state ID, student ID from a Kansas school, military ID, or passport all work. If you do not have an ID, you can get a free non-driver ID from the Kansas DMV.
📬
Kansas has no-excuse absentee voting — any registered voter can request a mail-in ballot without giving a reason. Request must be received by the county election office by the Tuesday before Election Day.
⏰
Polls are open from 7:00 AM to 7:00 PM on Election Day. If you are in line by 7:00 PM, you have the right to vote.
🗳️
Kansas has semi-closed primaries. In 2026, Republicans hold closed primaries (registered Republicans only). Democrats allow registered Democrats and unaffiliated voters to participate.
✅
Your right to vote is restored immediately after release from prison and completion of any probation or parole in Kansas. Pretrial detention and misdemeanor convictions do not restrict voting rights.
Four steps to vote
1
Check if you are already registered
Some Kansans are automatically registered when they get a driver's license or state ID. Check your status before assuming you are not registered.
🔗 Check your registration — voteks.org
2
Register (or update your registration)
You can register online, by mail, or in person at your county election office or the DMV. Remember: deadline is 21 days before Election Day.
🔗 Register — Kansas Secretary of State
3
Find out what is on your ballot
Your ballot has more on it than just big races. Look up your specific ballot before Election Day so you are not deciding cold in the voting booth.
🔗 Sample ballot lookup — Vote411.org
4
Find your polling place and go
Look up your assigned polling location in advance. Bring your photo ID. Arrive with time to spare. If you are in line by 7:00 PM you have the right to vote — do not leave.
🔗 Find your polling place — voteks.org
The next election in Kansas
There is almost always an election coming up — school boards, city councils, primaries, special elections. Most have turnout so low that a handful of people determine the outcome. Check what is coming up and make a plan.
